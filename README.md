Title: Regulatory Reform and the Conduct of Clinical Trials: Evidence from the 21st Century Cures Act

Abstract: In December 2016, the United States enacted the 21st Century Cures Act to accelerate medical innovation by expanding acceptable evidence and increasing flexibility in clinical trial design. This thesis investigates its causal impact on clinical trial characteristics and sponsor behaviour using a difference-in-differences framework applied to data from the Aggregate Analysis of ClinicalTrials.gov database. We find a statistically significant increase in trial duration, contrary to the Act’s intended objective. There is no evidence of effects on completion rates or enrollment sizes. At the sponsor level, increases in trial initiation under baseline specifications are not robust to alternative specifications. We also find heterogeneity in treatment effects: increases in trial duration are primarily driven by trials involving both U.S. and international sites, while no significant effects are observed for U.S.-only trials. Overall, the findings highlight that regulatory reforms may generate unintended consequences, reinforcing the value of evidence-based policy.

Keywords: 21st Century Cures Act, difference-in-differences, clinical trials, regulatory policy, trial duration, AACT
